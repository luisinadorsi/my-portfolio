## Anti-Patterns Verdict

**LLM assessment**: The site has genuine craft — the GSAP clip-path reveal, the fan photo interaction, the LetterSwap hero animation, the grain overlay, the diagonal section dividers. These save it from being generic. But the **foundational typography choice undermines everything else**. Cormorant Garamond paired with DM Sans is the single most common 2025 portfolio combination — both fonts appear on impeccable's reflex-reject list. The aesthetic lane (warm serif display + italic + small tracked uppercase labels + restrained muted palette) is the saturated editorial-typographic lane the skill explicitly flags. A recruiter who has reviewed 30 portfolios this year has seen this exact combination many times. The site's unique voice — GIS + architecture + emotion + sustainability — deserves typography that couldn't belong to a generic creative portfolio.

The palette itself is warm and appealing, but it's playing safe. The terracotta/sage/rose combination is beautiful but gentle — it doesn't commit to a POV the way brand.md recommends.

**Deterministic scan (detect.mjs)**: 1 finding — `transition: width` in `components/ui/card-stack.tsx:283` (layout-property animation, performance warning). No findings in the `app/` directory.

## Overall Impression

The bones are strong — real animation craft, genuine personality in the copy, smart layout choices. The photography fan interaction in the About section is a highlight. What's holding it back: opacity-based muted text that fails WCAG AA contrast across the board, a typography combination that signals "AI-assisted portfolio" to anyone who's seen enough of them, and a hover-dependent project reveal that leaves mobile users with an inferior experience. The single biggest opportunity: re-root the typography in something that actually reflects who Luisina is — a cartographer turned product designer. The font choice should make someone ask "what is that?" not "oh, another Cormorant portfolio."

## What's Working

**1. The BentoCard GSAP clip-path reveal.** The circle-expand-on-hover is genuinely distinctive — it's kinetic, spatial, and unexpected. It earns its weight and signals the designer knows motion. This is a keep.

**2. The fan photo interaction in About.** Spring physics, stacked polaroids, fanning on hover — this is specific, human, and delightful. It tells you something about Luisina that a headshot wouldn't. Strong.

**3. Token system architecture.** The `@theme` token structure in `globals.css` is clean and coherent. The palette names are intentional (`--color-terra`, `--color-sage`). The foundation is right — it's just not being used consistently everywhere.

## Priority Issues

### [P1] Both typefaces are on the reflex-reject list — editorial-typographic lane saturation
**Why it matters**: Cormorant Garamond + DM Sans is the most common 2025 design portfolio combination. It signals "I used a template" or "AI built this" to any recruiter who's seen more than a handful of portfolios. Luisina's actual identity — GIS, cartography, architecture, sustainability — is distinctive and deserves typography that couldn't belong to anyone else.
**Fix**: Replace both typefaces. The brief calls for "grounded, curious, generous" — think about a physical object this portfolio could be: a field surveyor's notebook, an architect's annotated section drawing, a naturalist's pressed-plant journal. Find display and body type from that object. Candidates to explore: Canela (Klim), Reforma (Type-together), Libre Caslon (warm editorial), Degular (neutral-warm geometric). For body: Hanken Grotesk, Figtree, Nunito, Sentient. Avoid any font on the reflex-reject list.
**Suggested command**: `/impeccable typeset`

### [P1] Opacity-based text fails WCAG AA contrast throughout
**Why it matters**: `text-[var(--color-text)]/50`, `/60`, `/65` appear across the hero tagline, About bio, project metadata, stats labels, and contact section. Computed contrast ratios: `/50` ≈ 2.1:1, `/60` ≈ 3.6:1, `/65` ≈ 4.2:1 — all fail the 4.5:1 AA minimum for normal-sized text. Users with low vision, or anyone reading on a phone in sunlight, cannot reliably read this content. The minimum passing opacity on this background is approximately `/70` (≈ 5.0:1). The 10px stats labels at `/50` opacity are especially problematic.
**Fix**: Replace all opacity-based muted text with dedicated solid CSS tokens. Add `--color-text-muted` (~`#6b5f59`, a darker warm gray) and `--color-text-subtle` (~`#856e66`) to the token system. Use these tokens instead of opacity utilities.
**Suggested command**: `/impeccable colorize`

### [P1] Project taglines are invisible on mobile (hover-only reveal)
**Why it matters**: The BentoCard clip-path reveal is desktop-only — on mobile, hover events don't fire, so the tagline ("What if your map knew how you felt?") is never visible. A recruiter opening the portfolio on their phone sees only the project title in small text at the bottom of each card. The tagline is the entire pitch for each project; hiding it from ~60% of mobile traffic is a critical content visibility failure.
**Fix**: Show the tagline statically on mobile below the card title, or restructure the mobile card to display title + tagline + first tag by default without requiring the hover state. The hover reveal can remain for desktop.
**Suggested command**: `/impeccable adapt`

### [P1] `height="100vh"` on the ArcGIS StoryMap iframe is a silent no-op
**Why it matters**: In `app/projects/from-maps-to-memories/page.tsx`, the iframe has `height="100vh"` as an HTML attribute. HTML iframe `height` only accepts pixel integers — "100vh" is ignored, leaving the iframe at its default height (150px). The `style` prop already sets `minHeight: '100vh'` which does work, but the dead `height` attribute creates confusion and the iframe behavior depends entirely on the CSS override.
**Fix**: Remove `height="100vh"` from the iframe attributes. The existing `style={{ minHeight: '100vh' }}` is sufficient.
**Suggested command**: `/impeccable polish`

### [P2] Hardcoded hex values bypass the token system (~20+ instances)
**Why it matters**: BentoCard's `accentHexMap`, `tagColorMap`, and `overlayTitleColor` default hardcode palette colors as hex strings instead of CSS variables. The same pattern repeats in page files (`#2d6b5a`, `#fdf8f5`, `#e8869a`, `#8fc4a0`, `#a8c9de`). If the palette ever shifts — even slightly — these values won't update and the design breaks silently. It also makes theming impossible.
**Fix**: Extend the token system with semantic aliases (`--color-overlay-title`, `--color-card-bg-teal`, etc.) and replace all hardcoded hex values in components with `var()` references or Tailwind token utilities.
**Suggested command**: `/impeccable harden`

## Persona Red Flags

**Jordan (First-timer recruiter at a sustainability org)**: Opens the portfolio on her phone from a LinkedIn message. Sees the hero — "Where data meets emotion." Great hook. Scrolls to the project grid. On mobile, she sees five cards with project titles at the bottom. The taglines — the actual reason to care about each project — are nowhere. She has no idea what MoodMaps does. She opens the MoodMaps page, scrolls the full case study. At the bottom, she wants to contact Luisina. The "Say hello." heading links to `mailto:luisina@example.com` — a placeholder address. She can't email. The contact strip has LinkedIn/Medium/Behance links but she needed email. She leaves.

**Casey (Mobile user, distracted, one-handed)**: The BentoCard tag labels (`paddingLeft: 110`, `fontSize: 10`) are extremely small — well below 44px touch target. The Stats row renders 3 columns on a 375px viewport — labels like "years designing systems that feel human" are unreadably truncated. The `circleFloat` animation on the decorative circles runs continuously with no reduced-motion override — a small drain but accumulates across the page.

**Sam (Keyboard + screen reader user)**: The BentoCard overlay is `aria-hidden="true"` so the tagline is invisible to screen readers — only the `aria-label="View MoodMaps case study"` on the Link is announced. This is actually correct behaviour (the decorative overlay is hidden), but it means the tagline is never announced to screen reader users either, same as the mobile problem. The skip link exists and works — that's a genuine positive.

## Minor Observations

- `circleFloat` and `blobBreathe` keyframe animations have no `@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)` override in `globals.css` — only `.fade-in` is covered. These are decorative but should respect the user preference.
- The StatsRow grid is `grid-cols-3` with no responsive variant — on a 375px screen, three columns with long text labels will either overflow or truncate.
- `card-stack.tsx:283` animates `width` as a CSS transition property — layout thrash warning from the detector. If this component is used, swap for a `transform: scaleX()` or `clip-path` approach.
- Contact email in the hero and footer is `luisina@example.com` — a placeholder. Needs updating before launch.
- The mobile hamburger menu closes on Esc (good) but not on backdrop click or on-link-click outside the hamburger element itself. After clicking a nav link, it closes — that part works, but clicking anywhere else in the open mobile page does not close it.
- The `heroFadeIn` CSS animation has no `prefers-reduced-motion` override — the hero heading animation will still play for users who've requested reduced motion. The JS FadeIn component handles this correctly; the CSS keyframe version doesn't.

## Questions to Consider

- What if the typography was drawn from cartographic instruments rather than editorial publishing? What font would appear in a 1970s Ordnance Survey map legend, an architect's title block, or a botanical field journal?
- The project cards hide their best copy (the tagline) until hover. What if the card's default state was already enough to make someone want to click — without needing the reveal?
- The palette is warm and safe. What if one section committed fully to a colour — not as accent, but as the entire background? The contact section in terracotta is close — but it's one section. What would happen if the hero was sage-drenched?
