# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Primarily recruiters and hiring managers at mission-driven product companies — urban planning, sustainability, healthcare, civic tech, architecture-adjacent. Occasionally freelance clients or collaborators. They open the portfolio from a LinkedIn message, a job application, or a referral. They are scanning fast: does this designer understand systems? Does her work feel relevant to us? Can I tell what she worked on and why it matters? They are not necessarily design experts — they may be PMs, founders, or architects who need to trust her craft without knowing how to articulate it.

## Product Purpose

Luisina's portfolio positions her as a designer at the intersection of GIS, architecture, and UX — someone who doesn't just make interfaces, but understands space, data, and human emotion in a unified way. The goal is to attract the right collaborators (curious, mission-driven, systems-minded), not to impress the widest audience. Success looks like: "I need to reach out to this person."

## Brand Personality

Warm, alive, deeply personal. Not a polished agency portfolio — the voice of someone who genuinely thinks about how spaces make people feel. The brand words are: **grounded, curious, generous**. The feeling it should leave: like finding a cartographer's field notebook on a shelf — handwritten, annotated, full of feeling, unmistakably made by one specific human.

## Anti-references

- Gaming aesthetics, neon-on-dark, loud startup energy
- Cold, minimal, "I went to a tech bootcamp" template portfolios
- Overly formal agency decks — the kind that could be from anyone
- PosterLad-style bold graphic aggression (too loud for the sustainability/wellbeing register)
- Glassmorphism, gradient mesh, frosted cards
- Generic "creative" Webflow templates with Cormorant + DM Sans (this exact combination is the single most common 2025 portfolio cliché — must differentiate)

## Design Principles

1. **Specific over generic.** The work spans GIS, architecture, healthcare, and emotion — that's a distinct combination. Every design choice should make that specificity visible, not smooth it over into generic editorial polish.
2. **Feeling is information.** The interface itself should demonstrate Luisina's thesis — that good design carries emotional weight. Not decorative warmth, but intentional emotional architecture.
3. **The map is the metaphor.** Her background in cartography and spatial thinking is the through-line. Layering, navigation, place, and annotation are native metaphors — lean into them rather than adopting default tech-portfolio conventions.
4. **Trust the recruiter's time.** The primary user is scanning. Make the essential message — who she is, what she does, what she's made — legible in under 30 seconds. Then reward those who dig deeper.
5. **Alive, not polished.** Perfection signals caution. The portfolio should feel like it was made with conviction, not committee approval. Take aesthetic risks that a Webflow template wouldn't.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. Reduced-motion support required (prefers-reduced-motion already partially implemented). Keyboard navigation for all interactive elements. Touch-target sizes ≥ 44px. Color contrast 4.5:1 for body text — currently several opacity-based text values fall below this threshold and need addressing.
